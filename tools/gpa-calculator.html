ℹ️ About This Tool

GPA (Grade Point Average) and CGPA (Cumulative Grade Point Average) are weighted averages of your grade points across subjects, where the weight of each subject is its credit hours. The formula is: GPA = Sum(Grade Points × Credits) / Sum(Credits). A subject worth more credits contributes more to your overall GPA than a low-credit subject.

India uses the UGC-mandated 10-point scale where O (Outstanding) = 10, A+ = 9, A = 8, B+ = 7, B = 6, C = 5, and below 5 is failing (P = 4). To convert CGPA to percentage under UGC guidelines, multiply by 9.5. The US 4-point system is also included for students applying abroad or enrolled in US-affiliated programs.

CGPA is your overall GPA across all semesters combined. To calculate it using this tool, enter all your subjects from all semesters together — the weighted average handles the rest. If you want per-semester GPA separately, calculate each semester individually and note the results.

Many Indian universities, PSU recruiters, and graduate school admissions use CGPA cutoffs. A common threshold is 6.0 CGPA on the 10-point scale (equivalent to approximately 57%) for engineering college placements, and 7.0 or above for many PSU/government jobs. Top graduate programs typically require 7.5+ CGPA.

How to use:

  1. Select the grade scale (10-point for India, 4-point for US).
  2. Add each subject with its grade points and credit hours.
  3. Click "Calculate GPA" to see your GPA, equivalent percentage, and total credits.

A common question is whether it's better to retake a course with a poor grade versus moving forward. This calculator helps answer that concretely: enter your current courses, then try a scenario where the poor-grade subject is replaced by a higher grade (keeping the same credits) and see the exact GPA impact. Given that credit hours weight the calculation, a poor grade in a high-credit core subject can drag down GPA significantly more than the same grade in a low-credit elective. Conversely, excelling in a high-credit subject can boost GPA disproportionately. This mathematical reality is useful when prioritizing study effort allocation — directing extra study time to high-credit subjects that you're borderline in yields greater GPA return than polishing already-high grades in low-credit courses. International university applications typically ask for transcripts in both letter grades and numeric GPA, so using the correct scale and conversion method for your target country's application portal avoids confusion during the application process.
