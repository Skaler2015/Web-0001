ℹ️ About This Tool

CTC (Cost to Company) and your actual in-hand salary are very different numbers, and the gap often surprises people when they receive their first payslip at a new job. CTC includes everything the company spends on you — your basic salary, HRA, allowances, the employer's PF contribution, gratuity provisions, and sometimes even subsidized meals or insurance premiums. What you actually receive in your bank account every month is your in-hand or take-home salary, after deducting your EPF contribution, professional tax, and income tax (TDS).

This calculator breaks down a CTC into its approximate components, calculates standard deductions, and estimates the actual monthly amount credited to your account. It's most useful for understanding what to expect from a salary offer before accepting it, for planning monthly budgets accurately, and for comparing multiple job offers that quote CTC differently.

How to use:

  1. Enter your annual CTC (the number in your offer letter).
  2. Set the basic salary percentage — most companies set it at 40–50% of CTC.
  3. Set the HRA percentage — usually 40% of basic for non-metro cities, 50% for metros.
  4. Enter the variable bonus percentage (if any) and your city's professional tax.
  5. Click "Calculate" to see your expected monthly take-home salary.

Why CTC and take-home differ so much

The main deductions are: EPF (your share of 12% of basic), professional tax (varies by state, typically ₹150–₹2500/month), and TDS (tax deducted at source based on estimated income tax). Additionally, the employer's PF contribution and gratuity provisions are included in CTC but never appear in your payslip or bank account — they're costs borne by the company on your behalf. Variable pay or bonus is not guaranteed monthly and is usually paid quarterly or annually.

Understanding your actual in-hand salary before accepting a job offer prevents a common disappointment: candidates sometimes accept a CTC that sounds attractive, only to find the monthly bank credit significantly lower than expected. This is especially common with high-bonus structures where a large proportion of CTC is variable and paid once or twice a year rather than monthly, or where the employer PF contribution is included in the CTC figure quoted in the offer letter.

Using this calculator before salary negotiations gives you a grounded view of the actual take-home impact of a proposed CTC increase, which helps you negotiate not just the headline CTC number but the specific structure — a higher basic (which increases PF and HRA) vs a higher variable (which is uncertain and taxed differently) — making it possible to advocate for a compensation structure that genuinely maximizes your monthly in-hand salary rather than just the total CTC on paper.
