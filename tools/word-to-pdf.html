ℹ️ About This Tool

The ApneSoftware Word to PDF tool converts Microsoft Word .docx documents into clean, portable PDF files, entirely inside your web browser and without uploading your document to any server. PDF is the universal format for sharing finished documents: it looks the same on every device, cannot be accidentally reflowed or edited, and is accepted everywhere for submissions, printing, archiving and official records. Word, by contrast, is an editing format whose appearance can shift between computers depending on the fonts and version installed. Converting to PDF fixes the layout in place. This tool does that conversion quickly and privately, and — unlike the most basic converters — it works hard to preserve the structure and formatting that make a document readable.

Under the hood it combines two trusted open‑source libraries. Mammoth.js reads the .docx file and interprets its semantic structure — which paragraphs are headings, which text is bold or italic, where the lists, links, images and tables are — and produces a clean representation of the document. pdf‑lib then lays that structure out into a real PDF, drawing genuine, selectable text rather than a flat picture. The result is a searchable, copyable PDF whose headings look like headings, whose lists are numbered or bulleted, whose bold and italic runs are preserved, and whose hyperlinks remain clickable. Because both libraries run as JavaScript in your browser, the whole process is instant and completely private.

A great deal of care goes into formatting fidelity. Word heading styles become properly sized PDF headings; ordered lists keep their numbering and unordered lists their bullets, with sensible indentation; inline bold and italic — including bold‑italic — are rendered with the matching font weights; blockquotes are indented; and horizontal rules are drawn. Hyperlinks are not merely coloured text: they are written as real PDF link annotations, so a reader can click them to open the target. Images embedded in the Word file are extracted and placed into the PDF at a sensible size, and tables are reconstructed as a bordered grid with the text wrapped inside each cell. Content flows naturally across pages, with automatic page breaks so nothing is clipped.

One of the most important capabilities is genuine Hindi and Unicode support. Many simple converters can only handle basic English (Latin‑1) text and produce blank boxes or errors when they meet Devanagari, Tamil, Bengali, Arabic or other scripts, or even a rupee sign. This tool detects when your document contains non‑Latin characters and automatically embeds the appropriate Noto font so those characters render correctly and remain selectable in the finished PDF. That makes it genuinely useful for Indian users converting Hindi resumes, letters, notices and study material, as well as for anyone working in a multilingual document.

You also control how the output looks. Before converting you can choose the page size (A4, Letter or Legal), the orientation (portrait or landscape), the margins (narrow, normal or wide) and the base font size, so the PDF matches your intended use — a tight single‑page letter, a wide landscape table, or a spacious printed report. You can convert a single file or drop in several .docx files at once; the tool processes them one after another with a progress bar and lets you download them individually or bundled into a single ZIP. A built‑in preview renders the first page and reports the page count so you can check the result before downloading, and the output PDF carries sensible metadata derived from the file.

It helps to understand the two broad ways software turns Word into PDF, because they suit different needs. One approach photographs each page — it renders the document exactly as one program displays it and saves that picture, reproducing every visual detail but producing a heavier file whose text cannot be selected or searched. The other approach, which this tool uses, rebuilds the document from its underlying structure into fresh, native PDF content. The advantage is a light, fully selectable, searchable and accessible PDF whose text can be copied, indexed by search engines and read aloud by screen readers; the trade‑off is that pixel‑exact reproduction of highly designed layouts is not guaranteed. For the vast majority of everyday documents — letters, resumes, notes, reports, assignments and articles that are mostly text with some headings, lists, links and the occasional image or table — the rebuild approach gives a cleaner, more useful result. For a brochure or form that depends on precise positioning, exporting straight from Microsoft Word remains the best match, and this page’s “Conversion Limitations” and “Troubleshooting” sections explain exactly what to expect.

Above all, this tool is private by design. Every step — reading the Word file, interpreting its structure, embedding fonts and images, and writing the PDF — happens entirely on your own device using mammoth.js and pdf‑lib. Nothing is uploaded to a server, nothing is stored, and no account or email is required; once the page has loaded you can even work offline. This matters because the documents people convert are often personal or confidential — resumes with contact details, contracts, financial letters, medical notes, legal drafts — and they should never be sent to a stranger’s server merely to change their format. Whether you are a student converting an assignment, a professional turning a proposal into a shareable PDF, an office standardising its paperwork, or an individual preparing a Hindi document for printing, this tool gives you fast, faithful, private Word‑to‑PDF conversion with nothing to install.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

🔒 Privacy & Security

This tool converts your document entirely inside your browser using the mammoth.js and pdf‑lib libraries. Your .docx file is never uploaded to any server, never stored, and never transmitted anywhere. No account, email or signup is required, and once the page and fonts have loaded you can work offline. Because the documents people convert — resumes, contracts, letters, reports — often contain personal information, this local‑only design keeps them under your sole control from upload to download.

🌐 Browser Compatibility

Works in all modern browsers — Google Chrome, Mozilla Firefox, Microsoft Edge, Safari, Brave and Opera — on Windows, macOS, Linux, Android and iOS. It relies only on JavaScript and the standard File, Blob and Canvas APIs. On phones and tablets the layout stacks into a single column with touch‑friendly controls. Converting documents that use non‑Latin scripts downloads a Unicode font once; after that it is cached for the session.

📄 Supported File Formats

Input: Microsoft Word .docx files (Word 2007 and later — the Office Open XML format). The legacy binary .doc, and .rtf, .odt and .dot/.dotx formats are not read directly; open them in Word or a compatible editor and “Save As .docx” first. Output: a standard PDF with selectable text, or a ZIP of PDFs when you convert several files.

🧷 Supported PDF Standards

The tool generates standard PDF 1.7 documents with selectable, searchable text, embedded subset fonts (including Unicode Noto fonts where needed for Hindi and other scripts), embedded raster images and real link annotations for hyperlinks. The output opens in Adobe Reader, Chrome, Edge, Preview and every conforming PDF viewer.

⚠️ Conversion Limitations

Because it rebuilds the document rather than photographing it, the output focuses on faithful content and structure rather than pixel‑perfect reproduction. Preserved: headings, paragraphs, bold/italic, ordered/unordered lists, blockquotes, hyperlinks, images and simple tables, plus Unicode text. Simplified or not carried over: exact fonts and colours, multi‑column page layouts, text boxes, SmartArt, charts, WordArt, precise line/paragraph spacing, headers/footers, page numbers and watermarks, and complex table features such as merged cells. For documents that depend on such elements, exporting to PDF directly from Microsoft Word will match the original most closely.

🛠️ Troubleshooting

“Only .docx is supported.” Your file is likely a .doc, .rtf or .odt — open it in Word and Save As .docx. Hindi shows as boxes or fails: the Unicode font may not have loaded — check your connection and try again, as the font downloads once from a CDN. Layout looks different from Word: that is expected for complex layouts (see Conversion Limitations); adjust the page size and margins, or export from Word for an exact copy. A large file is slow: conversion runs locally, so very large documents with many images take longer — give it a moment, and the progress bar will show it working.
