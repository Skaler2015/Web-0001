ℹ️ About This Tool

Simple Interest (SI) is the most basic form of interest calculation, where interest is computed only on the original principal amount and not on any accumulated interest. The formula is SI = P × R × T, where P is the principal, R is the annual interest rate as a decimal, and T is the time in years. The total amount at the end is A = P + SI.

Simple interest is used in short-term loans, some personal loans, and a few bank products. It is also the standard taught in school mathematics. Unlike compound interest, simple interest grows linearly — doubling the time exactly doubles the interest, and doubling the rate exactly doubles the interest too. This predictability makes it easy to calculate mentally for quick estimates.

For example, ₹1,00,000 at 8% simple interest for 2 years gives SI = 1,00,000 × 0.08 × 2 = ₹16,000, making the total ₹1,16,000. The same amount at compound interest (quarterly) would grow to about ₹1,17,166 — slightly higher, which is why most bank savings products use compound interest.

This calculator is useful for students practicing SI/CI problems, for verifying a lender's interest quote on a personal loan, or for understanding the difference between simple and compound interest over different time periods.

How to use:

  2. Simple interest and total amount are shown instantly.

One practical scenario where SI appears regularly is in moneylender loans and certain informal credit arrangements in rural India, where lenders sometimes quote monthly simple interest rates rather than annual rates. Multiplying the monthly rate by 12 gives the annual rate for comparison with bank products. For example, a 2% monthly SI rate equals 24% annually — vastly higher than any bank personal loan rate, which highlights the importance of always converting quoted rates to a common annual basis before comparing. This calculator works with annual rates, so if your lender quotes monthly, multiply by 12 before entering it here. Treasury bills and some government bonds use simple interest for short-duration securities, which is another context where being able to quickly compute SI matters for comparing yields across different financial instruments available in the market. For academic purposes, SI problems commonly appear in bank exams like IBPS, SBI PO, SSC CGL and RRB, often asking to find either the principal, rate, or time given the others — this calculator helps verify solutions and build intuition for those problem types quickly.
