ℹ️ About This Tool

The ApneSoftware Unit Converter is a professional, all-in-one measurement conversion tool designed to replace the scattered, single-purpose converters most people juggle every day. Instead of one page for centimetres to inches, another for kilograms to pounds and yet another for Celsius to Fahrenheit, this single tool covers more than eighteen categories and over two hundred units — from everyday length, weight and temperature to advanced categories such as pressure, energy, power, data storage, data transfer, fuel economy, typography, frequency and angle. Whether you are a student checking physics homework, an engineer working across metric and imperial drawings, a cook following an American recipe with a European scale, a developer sizing files and bandwidth, or a traveller making sense of road signs and weather forecasts, the converter gives you an accurate answer the moment you type.

Unit conversion sounds simple, but doing it reliably by hand is surprisingly error-prone. Linear conversions — like metres to feet or litres to gallons — only need a multiplication factor, yet remembering the exact factor (a foot is 0.3048 metres, not 0.3) is where mistakes creep in. Other conversions are genuinely tricky: temperature uses an offset as well as a scale, so Celsius to Fahrenheit is not a single multiplication but °F = °C × 9/5 + 32. Fuel economy is even less intuitive because litres-per-100-kilometres is an inverse measure — a smaller number is better, and converting it to miles-per-gallon means dividing rather than multiplying. This tool handles all of these correctly behind the scenes, converting every value through a well-defined base unit for each category so the result is mathematically exact, not a rough approximation.

Transparency is a core design goal. Every conversion shows the formula or factor it used, so you can see why the answer is what it is, copy that formula into your notes, and build an intuition for how units relate. Alongside the main result, the tool always displays the reverse conversion and a full conversion table that shows your value expressed in every unit of the category at once. That table is perfect for quick reference — enter “1 metre” and instantly see it in millimetres, centimetres, kilometres, inches, feet, yards, miles and nautical miles. You can export the whole table as CSV or Excel, or print it and save as PDF, making the tool useful for reports, lab sheets, shipping documents and classroom handouts.

The interface is built for speed and comfort. A category chip bar and dropdown let you switch measurement types instantly; a unit search box filters long lists so you can jump straight to “nautical mile” or “kilopascal”; a swap button reverses the direction in one click; and a precision selector, scientific-notation toggle and thousands-separator option let you format results exactly how you need them — two decimals for a recipe, ten for engineering, or exponential notation for astronomical and microscopic values. Frequently used conversions can be saved as favourites, and your recent history is kept so you can re-open a previous conversion with a single click. All of this state is stored locally in your browser, so it is there when you come back but never sent anywhere.

Privacy and reliability matter as much as features. The converter is entirely client-side: the numbers you enter are processed by JavaScript running in your own browser and are never uploaded to any server. There is no account, no tracking of your inputs, and no network round-trip, which also means the tool is fast and keeps working offline once loaded. The unit database is lightweight and the calculations are pure arithmetic, so results appear instantly even on modest phones. Accessibility has been considered throughout — controls are keyboard-navigable, labelled for screen readers, show clear focus outlines, and respect high-contrast preferences — and the responsive layout adapts cleanly from large desktop monitors down to small mobile screens with touch-friendly targets. In short, it is a converter you can trust for quick everyday questions and for precise professional work alike, all from a single, private, always-available page.

The tool is also designed to grow and stay maintainable. Every category is described by a small, readable data table that maps each unit to a single conversion factor (or, for special cases like temperature and fuel economy, a pair of functions), so adding a new unit or an entire new category is a one-line change with no risk to the rest of the engine. The same base-unit approach that guarantees accuracy also keeps the codebase compact and easy to audit — an important quality for a tool people rely on for schoolwork, engineering sign-off, laboratory records and commercial documentation. Common real-world tasks are covered out of the box: converting cm to inches for online shopping, kg to pounds for fitness and shipping, Celsius to Fahrenheit for weather and recipes, km to miles for travel, litres to gallons for fuel, square feet to square metres for property, GB to MB for files and storage, and Mbps to MB/s for download speeds — each with the formula shown so you always understand the result rather than just trusting a black box.

🧮 Conversion Formula

Every category defines a single base unit. To convert, the tool first converts your value into the base unit, then out of the base unit into the target — guaranteeing consistent, exact results no matter which two units you pick.

Linear conversions (length, weight, volume, data, etc.)

base value = input × factor(from)  →  result = base value ÷ factor(to). For example, 1 foot = 0.3048 m and 1 inch = 0.0254 m, so 1 ft = 0.3048 ÷ 0.0254 = 12 in.

Temperature (offset-based)

°F = °C × 9/5 + 32  •  K = °C + 273.15  •  °C = (°F − 32) × 5/9  •  °R = (°C + 273.15) × 9/5.

Fuel economy (inverse)

Values pass through kilometres per litre (km/L). Because litres-per-100 km is inverse, L/100km = 100 ÷ (km/L) and km/L = 100 ÷ (L/100km). 1 US mpg ≈ 0.4251 km/L.

⚠️ Limitations

  • Currency is not included. Exchange rates change constantly and require a live network feed, which would break the tool's offline, no-server privacy promise — use a dedicated currency tool for that.
  • Floating-point precision. Like all computers, results use IEEE-754 double precision; extremely large or tiny values may show tiny rounding artefacts. Use scientific notation for such ranges.
  • Approximate units. Some units are inherently approximate or context-dependent (a “month” is taken as 30 days, a “cup” differs between US and metric, Mach depends on altitude/temperature). Units are labelled to make the standard used clear.
  • Cooking is by volume, not weight. Volume-to-weight for ingredients (e.g. a cup of flour in grams) depends on density and is outside the scope of a pure unit converter.

🛠️ Troubleshooting

  • Result shows “—”: the value field is empty or not a number. Enter a numeric value (you can use a leading minus for negative temperatures).
  • A unit is missing from the list: clear the search box — it filters the units shown in a category.
  • Numbers look too long or too short: change the Precision selector or toggle Scientific notation.
  • Copy or export didn't work: some browsers block clipboard/downloads until you interact with the page; click once and try again, or use Print / PDF.
  • Favourites/history disappeared: they are stored in your browser; private/incognito mode or clearing site data will reset them.
