⚙️ How it works

When you add a PDF, the tool reads it entirely in memory and uses Mozilla's PDF.js engine to render each selected page onto an HTML5 canvas at your chosen DPI. Those page canvases are then composed onto one output canvas according to your layout — stacked vertically, laid side by side, or arranged in a grid — with your margins, background, borders and watermark applied. The result is encoded into your chosen image format and offered for download. For JPG, PNG and WebP the browser's native encoder is used; BMP and TIFF are written by lightweight built-in encoders, so no external service is ever involved.

📁 Supported formats

Input is any standard PDF. Output can be JPG (small, universal, no transparency), PNG (lossless with transparency), WebP (modern, smaller than both), BMP (uncompressed, maximally compatible) or TIFF (lossless, favoured in print and archival workflows). JPG and BMP are always opaque; PNG, WebP and TIFF preserve a transparent background when you choose one.

🎚️ Quality options

Quality is governed by two independent controls. DPI/scale sets how finely each PDF page is rasterised — higher DPI means sharper text and larger files, ideal for printing, while lower DPI keeps files light for chat and web. The quality slider then controls the compression level for JPG and WebP output. Combined with the width presets, you can dial in exactly the balance of sharpness and file size your destination needs.

ℹ️ About This Tool

The Professional PDF to Long Image Converter is a free, browser-based tool that transforms a PDF document into a single shareable image, or a set of them, exactly the way you want. It began as a simple utility that stacked every page into one tall picture — handy for WhatsApp and social media — and grew into a full conversion studio because turning a document into an image is rarely one-size-fits-all. Sometimes you want a long vertical scroll; sometimes a wide strip, a tidy grid, or a printable contact sheet. Sometimes you need a tiny JPG for a chat, and sometimes a lossless TIFF for archiving. This tool covers that whole range in one place, and it does everything locally so your document is never uploaded.

At its core the converter uses Mozilla's PDF.js, the same open-source engine that powers PDF viewing in modern browsers, to render each page of your file onto a canvas. Rendering happens at a DPI you choose, which is the single biggest factor in how the final image looks: a low setting produces small, web-friendly files, while a high setting yields crisp, print-quality output at the cost of size. Because rendering is done page by page with a live progress bar showing the percentage complete, an estimate of the time remaining, the running output size and the processing speed, even large, image-heavy PDFs remain manageable and you always know how far along the job is.

Once the pages are rendered, the layout engine takes over. The default vertical mode stitches pages top to bottom into the classic long image, ideal for reading in a messaging app where people naturally scroll. The horizontal mode places pages side by side for wide screens and timelines. The grid mode arranges pages in two to five columns, which is perfect for an at-a-glance overview or for printing several pages on one sheet, and the contact-sheet mode adds page-number labels to a thumbnail grid so you can index a long document quickly. Around these layouts you have precise control over the presentation: adjustable margins and a gap between pages, a background that can be white, black, transparent, a custom colour or a two-colour gradient, and per-page borders with a chosen thickness, colour, rounded corners and an optional drop shadow.

Getting exactly the pages you want, in the order you want, is just as flexible. You can include all pages, only the odd or even ones, just the first or last page, or a custom range such as "1-3, 5, 8". The order can be normal or reversed, and every page can be rotated by 90, 180 or 270 degrees to correct scans or fit a layout. For output you pick from five formats — JPG, PNG, WebP, BMP and TIFF — using the browser's own encoders for the first three and lightweight built-in encoders for BMP and TIFF, so nothing leaves your device. Preset widths make sizing effortless: choose the original resolution, a 1080-pixel width tuned for Instagram and WhatsApp, an A4 print width, or type a custom pixel width, and every page is scaled to match. A text watermark with adjustable opacity and a choice of centre, tiled or corner positions lets you brand or mark documents as confidential.

Two features make the tool genuinely comfortable with big jobs. First, long-image optimisation: a very tall stitched image can exceed the maximum canvas or image dimensions some apps accept, so you can set a maximum height and the tool will automatically split the output into several images, delivered together in a ZIP. Second, batch processing: you can add multiple PDFs to a queue and convert them in one run, again collecting the results in a ZIP for a single download. A live preview with zoom, pan, a full-screen view and a thumbnail strip lets you inspect the result before saving, and when you are happy you can download the image, copy it straight to your clipboard, print it, or use your device's native share sheet. A file-information panel keeps the essentials in view — source file size, total pages, page dimensions and the final output size.

Throughout, the design goals are speed, privacy and reach. The interface is responsive and touch-friendly, so it works on a phone with pinch-zoom as comfortably as on a desktop; the controls are labelled and keyboard-operable for accessibility; and because all processing is client-side, the tool is safe for confidential material and continues to work offline once loaded. There is no account, no watermark unless you add one yourself, and no artificial limits. Whether you are a student sharing lecture notes, a shop owner posting a menu, an office worker circulating a notice, or anyone who simply finds an image easier to send than a PDF, this converter turns that everyday need into a couple of clicks — while keeping your document entirely in your hands.

🛠️ Troubleshooting

  • The image is blank or an app won't open it: it may be taller than the app's limit — enable auto-split or reduce DPI/width.
  • Text looks blurry: increase the DPI/scale, or pick a larger width preset.
  • The file is too large to share: use JPG or WebP, lower the quality slider, and reduce the DPI.
  • Some pages are missing: check the page-selection and range settings.
  • A password-protected PDF won't load: remove the password in a PDF tool first, then convert.
  • The browser slows on a huge PDF: lower the DPI, convert a page range, or enable auto-split to cap memory use.
