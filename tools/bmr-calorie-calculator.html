🧬 One calculator for calories, macros and body composition

The Professional Health & Nutrition Calculator turns a basic BMR estimate into a complete daily-nutrition dashboard. From your age, height, weight, gender and activity level it calculates your Basal Metabolic Rate and Total Daily Energy Expenditure, then translates those numbers into calorie targets for every goal, a full macronutrient breakdown, your BMI and body-fat percentage, a personalised water-intake figure and a suggested meal distribution — with clear charts and one-click export. It is built for students, fitness enthusiasts, gym users, athletes and nutrition coaches, and it runs entirely in your browser so none of your health data is ever uploaded.

📐 Formula used

BMR can be estimated several ways, and this tool offers seven well-known formulas so you can match the method to your situation:

Mifflin-St Jeor : 10·kg + 6.25·cm − 5·age + (male +5 / female −161) Harris-Benedict : male 66.47 + 13.75·kg + 5.003·cm − 6.755·age female 655.1 + 9.563·kg + 1.850·cm − 4.676·age Katch-McArdle : 370 + 21.6·LBM (LBM = lean body mass in kg) Cunningham : 500 + 22·LBM Owen : male 879 + 10.2·kg / female 795 + 7.18·kg TDEE = BMR × activity multiplier (1.2 sedentary … 2.1 athlete)

Mifflin-St Jeor is the modern default and works well for most people. Katch-McArdle and Cunningham are more accurate for lean, muscular individuals because they use lean body mass (from your body-fat %). BMI uses weight ÷ height², and body fat uses the US-Navy circumference method.

📗 Healthy weight guide

BMI bands (WHO): under 18.5 is underweight, 18.5–24.9 is the healthy range, 25–29.9 is overweight and 30+ is obese. BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnosis — very muscular people can read as "overweight" despite low body fat, which is exactly why this calculator also shows body-fat percentage and lean mass. Aim for gradual change: roughly a 300–500 kcal daily deficit for sustainable fat loss (about 0.25–0.5 kg per week) or a similar modest surplus for lean muscle gain, paired with adequate protein and strength training.

ℹ️ About This Tool

The Professional Health & Nutrition Calculator is a free, browser-based tool that brings together the core numbers people need when they start paying attention to their nutrition and fitness. It began as a simple BMR and calorie calculator and grew into a complete dashboard because those figures are far more useful together than in isolation: knowing your maintenance calories only helps if you can also see how to split them into protein, carbohydrates and fat, how they change for a fat-loss or muscle-gain goal, how much water to drink, and where you stand on BMI and body fat. This tool computes all of that from a handful of inputs, instantly and privately, and presents it with charts and a meal breakdown so the numbers translate into something you can actually act on.

The foundation is your Basal Metabolic Rate — the energy your body uses at complete rest to breathe, circulate blood, regulate temperature and keep your organs working. Because no single equation is perfect for everyone, the calculator offers seven established formulas. The default, Mifflin-St Jeor, is widely regarded as the most accurate general-purpose equation for the modern population. Harris-Benedict and its revised version are long-standing alternatives. Owen and Schofield offer simpler estimates, while Katch-McArdle and Cunningham take a different and often more accurate route for lean, muscular people by basing the calculation on lean body mass derived from your body-fat percentage rather than total weight. Being able to choose the method — and to compare them — is part of what makes this a professional-grade tool rather than a one-formula widget.

Once your BMR is known, the tool multiplies it by an activity factor to estimate your Total Daily Energy Expenditure, the calories you actually burn on a typical day including movement and exercise. Eight activity levels are provided, from sedentary through to a two-a-day athlete, plus a physical-job option and a custom multiplier for people who know their own figure. From this maintenance number the calculator derives a full spectrum of calorie targets: mild, moderate and aggressive deficits for weight loss, the maintenance figure itself, and modest surpluses for lean gain and muscle building. It also expresses your intake as weekly, monthly and annual totals, which puts the daily number in a longer-term perspective that many people find motivating.

Calories alone do not build a diet, so the calculator breaks your chosen goal's target into macronutrients. Protein is set from your body weight and goal — higher for fat loss and muscle gain to preserve and build muscle — while fat is allocated a healthy share of total energy and carbohydrates fill the remainder, the balance that best supports training and recovery. It also estimates fiber and a sensible upper limit for added sugar, and calculates a personalised daily water target with adjustments for exercise and hot weather. A macronutrient pie chart makes the protein-carb-fat split obvious at a glance, and a meal-planning panel distributes the day's calories across breakfast, lunch, dinner and snacks so you have a realistic template to build meals around.

Body composition rounds out the picture. The tool shows your BMI with its category and the healthy weight range for your height, and — if you enter a few tape-measure circumferences — estimates body-fat percentage using the US-Navy method, along with your lean mass and fat mass in the same units you are working in. This matters because BMI on its own can mislabel muscular people; seeing body fat alongside it gives a truer sense of health and progress. Everything works in both metric and imperial units, switching instantly without losing your data, and a calorie-comparison chart lets you see loss, maintenance and gain targets side by side.

Just as important is what the tool does not do. It never sends your age, weight, measurements or goals anywhere — every calculation runs locally in your browser, so you can check your numbers as often as you like in complete privacy, and it keeps working offline once loaded. You can save results, recall recent ones and export a summary as CSV, Excel or PDF, or print and copy it, which is handy for coaches tracking clients or for keeping your own records over time. Inputs are validated to catch empty, zero or out-of-range values before they produce a nonsensical result. Above all, the calculator is deliberately conservative and honest: it favours gradual, sustainable targets over extreme deficits, and it repeats a clear medical disclaimer, because these are educational estimates from population-average formulas, not a substitute for personalised advice from a doctor or registered dietitian. Used consistently over weeks and months, and adjusted against your real-world progress on the scale, in the mirror and in the gym, it becomes a dependable reference point that quietly keeps your nutrition, hydration and training goals aligned — which is exactly what a good calculator should do.

What is the difference between BMR and TDEE?

BMR is the calories your body burns at complete rest; TDEE is BMR plus the calories from daily activity and exercise. / BMR आराम में जली calories है; TDEE में activity भी जुड़ती है।

Mifflin-St Jeor for most people; Katch-McArdle or Cunningham if you know your body-fat % and are lean/muscular. / अधिकतर के लिए Mifflin-St Jeor; lean लोगों के लिए Katch-McArdle।

🛠️ Troubleshooting

  • Results show a dash: a required field (age, height or weight) is empty or out of range — check the highlighted input.
  • Body fat shows "—": the US-Navy method needs neck, waist and height (plus hip for women); fill those in the measurements section.
  • Katch-McArdle/Cunningham look off: they need a body-fat %. Enter it, or the tool estimates it from your measurements.
  • Numbers seem too high: your activity level may be overstated — pick a lower level or a custom multiplier.
  • Imperial values look wrong: after switching units, re-check that height (inches) and weight (lb) reflect your real figures.
  • Export didn't download: allow downloads for this site and try again.
