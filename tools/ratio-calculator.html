ℹ️ About This Tool

Ratios describe the relationship between two quantities and come up constantly in everyday life — recipes scale up or down by ratio, map scales are ratios, financial ratios describe company performance, aspect ratios define screen proportions, and dividing profits or inheritance "in the ratio 2:3" requires ratio arithmetic. This tool handles three common ratio tasks: simplifying a ratio to its lowest terms, solving for a missing value in a proportion, and dividing a total amount in a given ratio.

Simplifying a ratio works exactly like simplifying a fraction — both numbers are divided by their greatest common divisor (GCD) to find the simplest equivalent form. The proportion solver (A:B = C:?) uses cross-multiplication to find the missing value. The ratio divider splits any total into parts proportional to the given ratio, showing the exact share for each part.

How to use:

  1. Choose the mode you need using the tabs at the top.
  2. Fill in the known values.
  3. Click the action button to get your result instantly.

Real-world examples

Simplify 24:36 → 2:3. Solve: if a recipe uses 3 cups flour for 4 servings, how much for 12 servings? (Answer: 9 cups — use the proportion solver with A=3, B=4, C=9). Divide ₹10,000 between partners in ratio 3:2 → Partner A gets ₹6,000, Partner B gets ₹4,000. These calculations appear in school math, business negotiations, cooking, map reading, and many other everyday situations requiring proportional reasoning.

Ratios are also central to financial analysis — the price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio, debt-to-equity ratio, and current ratio are all used by investors to evaluate company health. In chemistry, mixing solutions in specific ratios is essential for experiments. In photography and design, aspect ratios (16:9, 4:3) define how images are composed and displayed. Wherever two quantities need to be maintained in a specific proportional relationship, ratio arithmetic becomes an essential everyday skill across professional and academic contexts alike.

In the context of competitive exams and school curricula in India, ratio and proportion problems are consistently high-weight topics in mathematics sections of SSC, UPSC, CAT, and other standardized tests. The ability to quickly simplify ratios, solve proportion problems, and divide quantities in a given ratio is tested in time-pressured settings where manual calculation must be fast and accurate. This tool lets students verify their manual working, practice with different values, and build intuitive understanding of how ratios behave before relying solely on them in an exam setting.
