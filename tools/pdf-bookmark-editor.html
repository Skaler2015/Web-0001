⚙️ How it works

When you open a PDF, the tool loads it with two engines: PDF.js reads the existing outline (resolving each bookmark to a page), renders page thumbnails and extracts text for the auto-TOC, while pdf-lib is used to write the finished outline back into the file. You edit the bookmark tree on screen — titles, pages, nesting, destinations and styles — and when you are done, the tool builds a proper PDF outline dictionary, preserving the document's existing content and metadata, and hands you the updated file. Nothing is rasterised and the original pages are untouched, so the result is a clean PDF with working navigation.

🌳 Bookmark tree

The heart of the tool is an editable tree. Each row is a bookmark with a title and target page; the indentation shows its level. Use Indent and Outdent to make a bookmark a child of the one above or promote it back up, and Move up/down to reorder within a level. Add child creates a sub-bookmark, Duplicate copies a branch, and Delete removes it. Every change is recorded so Undo and Redo always have your back.

📑 TOC editor

Building a table of contents by hand is optional. The Auto-TOC feature scans the document's text with PDF.js, measures font sizes, and treats the larger, heading-like lines — and lines that match chapter or section numbering such as "Chapter 3" or "2.1" — as bookmarks, assigning each to the page it appears on and nesting sub-headings under their chapters. You can then tidy the result in the tree, rename entries, adjust levels and fix any page that needs a nudge.

🧷 Nested bookmarks

Real documents have structure — parts, chapters, sections and sub-sections — and this editor represents all of it. There is no depth limit: a section can contain sub-sections which contain their own points, and the collapsible tree keeps even a large outline manageable. When you export, the nesting is written as a genuine hierarchical PDF outline, so readers see expandable triangles in their bookmark panel exactly as they would in a professionally produced document.

🔁 Import & export

Your outline is portable. Export it as JSON (full structure with levels and styles), CSV (a simple spreadsheet of title, page and level), XML or a plain indented TXT list. You can edit these files elsewhere and import them back, or reuse one document's outline as a template for another. This makes it easy to standardise the table of contents across a series of reports or manuals.

Click any thumbnail to jump to that page, or click a bookmark's page field to preview exactly where it points — a quick way to confirm each destination is correct before you export. The validation panel flags empty titles, duplicate titles and pages that fall outside the document.

ℹ️ About This Tool

The Professional PDF Bookmark & TOC Editor is a free, browser-based tool for managing the navigation structure of a PDF — the bookmarks, also known as the outline or table of contents, that appear in the side panel of a PDF reader and let a person jump instantly to a chapter or section. A long PDF without bookmarks is genuinely hard to use: readers are left scrolling or guessing at page numbers, and there is no sense of the document's shape. This editor fixes that. It started as a simple tool that added a flat list of bookmarks and has grown into a full outline-management workspace that can detect what is already there, build a deeply nested structure, generate a table of contents automatically, and export the whole thing for reuse — all without your file ever leaving your device.

The tool is built on two complementary engines. PDF.js, the open-source viewer that powers PDF rendering in modern browsers, is used to read the document: it extracts the existing outline and resolves each bookmark to a real page, renders the thumbnails and current-page preview, and pulls out the text — complete with font sizes — that powers the automatic table-of-contents feature. pdf-lib, a pure-JavaScript PDF library, is used to write: it takes the tree you have built and constructs a correct, hierarchical PDF outline dictionary inside the file. Because the original page content is never touched, the exported PDF keeps its exact appearance and metadata and simply gains — or updates — a working bookmark panel.

Editing happens in an interactive tree. Every bookmark is a row with a title and a target page, and its indentation shows where it sits in the hierarchy. You can add a bookmark, add a child beneath the selected one, duplicate a branch, delete it, and move it up or down among its siblings; indent and outdent turn a top-level entry into a sub-section or promote it back. There is no limit on nesting depth, so the outline of a book with parts, chapters, sections and sub-points is represented faithfully, and a collapsible display keeps even a very large tree readable. Crucially, every action is captured in a history stack, so unlimited undo and redo mean you can experiment freely without fear of losing work.

Where a bookmark actually takes the reader is more nuanced than a page number, and the editor exposes that control. For each bookmark you choose a destination type — fit the page width, fit its height, show it at actual size, or use a custom zoom — and a position, so a bookmark can land at the top of a page, its middle, or a precise point. This is exactly the level of control that professional documents use to make navigation feel deliberate rather than approximate. You can also style each title in bold or italic and give it a colour, which readers' bookmark panels display, helping to visually separate parts from chapters or highlight important entries.

For long documents, creating dozens of bookmarks by hand would be tedious, so the tool can generate a table of contents automatically. The Auto-TOC feature reads the document's text through PDF.js, measures the font size of every line, and treats the larger, heading-like lines as bookmarks — nesting smaller sub-headings beneath larger ones — while also recognising common chapter and section patterns such as "Chapter 5" or "3.2". Each detected heading is linked to the page it appears on. The result is a first draft of the outline that you can refine in the tree, which turns an hour of manual work into a few seconds followed by light editing. Your outline is also fully portable: export it as JSON to keep every detail, as CSV for a quick spreadsheet edit, or as XML or an indented TXT list, and import any of those back to restore or reuse a structure across documents.

Confidence and correctness are supported throughout. A live preview renders the current page beside a scrollable strip of thumbnails; click a thumbnail to preview that page, or click a bookmark's page to jump to its destination and confirm it points where you intended. A validation panel continuously checks for the mistakes that break navigation — empty titles, duplicate titles and pages that fall outside the document — so you catch them before exporting. When you are ready, you can either add your new bookmarks to whatever the PDF already contains or replace the existing outline entirely, and the finished file downloads immediately. Because all of this runs in the browser, it is private by design: confidential manuscripts, board reports and legal bundles are processed only on your machine, the tool keeps working offline once loaded, and it handles large documents — hundreds or thousands of pages, and long outlines — without uploading a single byte. It is a small addition to a PDF, but a working table of contents is often the difference between a document people struggle with and one they can actually use.

🛠️ Troubleshooting

  • Bookmarks don't show in my reader: open the bookmark/outline side panel — many readers hide it by default.
  • Auto-TOC missed headings: the PDF may be a scan with no text, or headings aren't larger than body text — add them manually or run PDF OCR first.
  • A bookmark jumps to the wrong page: check its page number; remember the first page is page 1.
  • Existing bookmarks weren't detected: the PDF may have none, or an unusual outline — build a new one and tick "Replace existing".
  • The PDF won't load: it may be password-protected — unlock it first with PDF Unlock.
  • The browser slows on a huge PDF: thumbnails render on demand; scroll gradually and avoid generating thousands of bookmarks at once.
