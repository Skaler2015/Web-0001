ℹ️ About This Tool

The break-even point is the level of sales at which your total revenue exactly equals your total costs — meaning you're making neither a profit nor a loss. Every unit sold beyond this point generates pure profit, while selling fewer units results in a loss. Understanding your break-even point is one of the most fundamental pieces of financial analysis for any business, product launch, or pricing decision.

This calculator uses the standard break-even formula: Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price per Unit − Variable Cost per Unit). The denominator — Selling Price minus Variable Cost — is called the contribution margin, which represents how much each unit sold actually contributes toward covering your fixed costs before any profit begins.

How to use:

  1. Enter your total fixed costs — rent, salaries, equipment, subscriptions that don't change regardless of how much you sell.
  2. Enter the price at which you sell each unit of your product or service.
  3. Enter the variable cost per unit — raw materials, packaging, shipping, commissions that scale directly with sales.
  4. Click "Calculate Break-Even" to see the minimum units needed and the corresponding revenue target.

Why break-even analysis matters

Break-even analysis answers critical questions: Is this product profitable at this price point? How much do I need to sell before I start making money? What happens to profitability if my variable costs increase? It's a standard step in any business plan, investment proposal, or pricing review, and helps ensure decisions are grounded in realistic financial expectations rather than optimistic assumptions about sales volume.

For startups and small businesses, the break-even point is also a key threshold for investors and lenders — a business that can articulate its break-even point clearly demonstrates financial literacy and planning maturity. It answers the investor question "when will you stop losing money?" with a concrete, data-backed answer rather than vague optimism, which significantly increases credibility in funding conversations and business plan presentations.

For any product or service business, break-even analysis is typically the very first financial calculation done before launch. Knowing this number sets a clear, concrete goal — not vague targets like "sell as much as possible," but a specific units-sold milestone that separates loss from profit. It also helps determine whether the business model is even viable at realistic sales volumes, preventing costly investment into ventures that structurally cannot turn profitable without unrealistically high sales. For startups seeking funding, it also provides the concrete milestone investors ask: "when does this business stop losing money?" with a specific, quantifiable answer.
