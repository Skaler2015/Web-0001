📐 Formula

Compound growth of a lump sum is:

A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n × t)   and for continuous compounding   A = P × e^(r × t)

where P is the principal, r the annual rate (as a decimal), n the number of compounding periods per year and t the number of years. A regular contribution (annuity) adds:

FV = PMT × [ (1 + i)^m − 1 ] / i

where i is the periodic rate and m the number of contributions. Derived metrics: CAGR = (Final ÷ Invested)^(1/t) − 1, Effective Annual Return = (1 + r/n)^n − 1, and Inflation-adjusted value = Final ÷ (1 + inflation)^t.

🧮 Financial examples

  • Lump sum: ₹1,00,000 at 8% compounded monthly for 10 years ≈ ₹2,21,964 — the interest (₹1,21,964) exceeds the principal.
  • SIP: ₹5,000/month at 12% for 15 years invests ₹9,00,000 and grows to roughly ₹25 lakh — the extra ~₹16 lakh is compounding at work.
  • Inflation: ₹10,00,000 growing at 12% for 10 years is ~₹31 lakh nominally, but only ~₹17 lakh in today's money at 6% inflation.

ℹ️ About This Tool

The Professional Financial Investment Calculator is a free, browser-based planning tool that grew out of a simple compound-interest calculator into a complete engine for modelling how money grows. Its purpose is to answer, quickly and honestly, the questions people actually ask about their savings and investments: how much will this become, how much do I need to invest to reach a goal, what will it really be worth after inflation and tax, and how does one option compare with another? It does all of this locally, in your browser, so you can experiment freely with real numbers without ever sending them anywhere.

At its core is a single, careful simulation engine. Rather than relying on one closed-form formula that only fits a lump sum, it steps through the investment month by month, applying growth at whatever compounding frequency you choose — daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, half-yearly, annually or even continuously — and adding your recurring contributions or subtracting your withdrawals exactly when they occur. This period-by-period approach is what lets one tool faithfully represent very different products. A fixed deposit is just a lump sum with no contributions; a recurring deposit or SIP is a stream of equal contributions; an SWP is a stream of withdrawals from an invested corpus; a PPF or NPS account is a yearly or monthly contribution compounded annually. By selecting an investment type at the top, the relevant inputs are pre-filled with sensible defaults, but every field stays editable so you are never boxed in.

The results are deliberately richer than a single final figure. Alongside the headline total investment, interest or wealth gain and final value, the tool shows your ROI, your CAGR (the smoothed annual growth rate of your money), the effective annual return (what your nominal rate actually earns once compounding is applied), and — crucially — the inflation-adjusted value and post-tax value. Nominal returns can be misleading: a corpus that looks impressive in future rupees may buy far less than you expect once inflation is accounted for, and gains are often taxable. By surfacing the real, spendable outcome next to the headline number, the calculator helps you make decisions on the figures that actually matter.

Understanding is easier when you can see it, so the engine feeds four chart views — an area chart, a line chart, a bar chart and a pie split of principal versus interest — all drawn directly on an HTML5 canvas with no external chart library, which keeps the page fast and fully offline-capable. Beneath the charts sits a year-by-year growth table listing, for each year, the amount invested so far, the interest accumulated, the running balance and the year-on-year growth percentage. That schedule can be exported as a CSV for a spreadsheet, an Excel file, a PDF snapshot, or saved as a PNG image of the chart, and the whole view can be sent to your printer. For advisors preparing client illustrations, or students documenting a project, this export path turns an on-screen estimate into a shareable artefact in a couple of clicks.

Beyond the main calculator, four focused modes handle the questions that pure forward projection cannot. Compare places two scenarios on the same axes so you can settle debates like SIP versus lump sum, twelve percent versus fourteen, or monthly versus quarterly compounding, with the difference shown in money rather than hand-waving. The Goal planner works backwards from a target: tell it you want one crore in fifteen years and it solves for the monthly investment, the time, or the rate you would need, using a numerical search over the same simulation engine so the answer is internally consistent. The Reverse calculator does the same for a lump sum, finding the principal, rate or time required to reach a future value. And the Inflation mode isolates the erosion of purchasing power, showing present value, future value, the real return and the rupee "inflation loss" over your horizon. Together these cover the full arc of everyday financial planning without ever needing a spreadsheet.

The tool also respects that money is personal. Everything runs client-side, so your amounts, rates and goals never leave your device; there is no account, no tracking of your figures and no server round-trip. It supports seven currencies with correct local formatting, remembers your recent and saved calculations in your own browser for quick recall, and validates inputs to catch empty, negative or out-of-range values before they produce nonsense. It is designed to be lightweight and responsive, working just as well on a phone during a bank visit as on a desktop, and to remain accessible with clear labels and keyboard-friendly controls. One important caveat applies to every projection here: it assumes a constant rate of return, which real markets and even fixed deposits do not guarantee over long periods. Treat the outputs as informed estimates for planning and comparison, and confirm current rates and tax rules with your bank, fund house or a qualified advisor before committing money.

What is the difference between simple and compound interest?

Simple interest is earned only on the principal; compound interest is earned on the principal plus previously accumulated interest, so it grows faster over time. / simple interest सिर्फ principal पर मिलता है; compound interest principal और जमा interest दोनों पर।

What is CAGR?

Compound Annual Growth Rate — the single smoothed annual rate at which your money grew from the invested amount to the final value. / CAGR वह एकसमान वार्षिक दर है जिस पर आपका पैसा बढ़ा।

What is effective annual return?

The actual yearly yield of a nominal rate once compounding is applied — for example 12% compounded monthly is about 12.68% effective. / compounding के बाद nominal rate की असली वार्षिक यील्ड।

How does the inflation adjustment work?

It divides the future value by (1 + inflation)^years to show the amount in today's purchasing power. / यह future value को (1+inflation)^years से भाग देकर आज की क्रय-शक्ति में दिखाता है।

It applies your tax rate to the gains (not the principal) to show an approximate post-tax value. Real tax rules vary by instrument and country. / यह gains पर आपकी tax दर लगाकर अनुमानित post-tax value दिखाता है।

🛠️ Troubleshooting

  • Results show a dash or zero: a field is empty or invalid — check for negative numbers or a rate above 100%.
  • The goal/reverse solver says "not reachable": the target can't be met with the other values (e.g. rate too low for the time) — increase the rate, contribution or duration.
  • Numbers look too high: confirm the compounding frequency and that the contribution frequency (per month/quarter/year) matches your intent.
  • Inflation-adjusted value seems low: that is expected — it shows today's purchasing power, which is much less than the nominal future figure over long horizons.
  • Export button did nothing: your browser may have blocked the download; allow downloads for this site and try again.
  • Currency symbol looks wrong: pick your currency from the dropdown at the top; formatting follows that currency's locale.
