ℹ️ About This Tool

The ApneSoftware EXIF Viewer & Remover is a professional, privacy-first utility for inspecting and stripping the hidden metadata that cameras and phones bury inside your photos. Almost every JPEG you take carries an EXIF block — a package of technical and personal data recorded at the moment of capture — and many images also carry GPS coordinates, IPTC and XMP descriptive fields, an embedded thumbnail and camera maker notes. Most people never see any of this, yet it travels with the file wherever it goes. When you post that photo online, you may be quietly sharing the exact latitude and longitude where it was taken, your camera's make, model and serial number, the precise date and time, editing software, and sometimes even a copyright owner or author name. This tool makes all of that visible in plain language, and then lets you remove it — cleanly and locally — before you share.

As a viewer, the tool goes well beyond the handful of tags a basic reader shows. It parses the complete EXIF set alongside GPS, IPTC, XMP, interoperability and thumbnail (IFD1) blocks, then presents every field in a searchable, filterable table grouped by category. A search box lets you jump to any tag by name, and category chips filter the view down to just EXIF, GPS, IPTC or XMP. Alongside the raw tags, an image information panel summarises the practical details — width and height, megapixels, DPI/resolution, colour space, MIME type and file size — so you can verify an image at a glance. If GPS data is present, a prominent alert shows the coordinates and links straight to the location on a map, so you can see for yourself exactly what a photo would reveal.

The tool turns that inspection into an actionable privacy score. Rather than leaving you to interpret dozens of cryptic tags, it rates each image as Low, Medium or High risk based on what it exposes — GPS location weighs heaviest, followed by device identifiers, timestamps, software and any owner or author fields — and lists the specific concerns as badges. This makes it immediately obvious when a picture is safe to share and when it needs cleaning. For those who want the details, everything can be exported as a report: plain TXT, structured JSON, spreadsheet-ready CSV, XML, or a printable PDF — useful for documentation, audits, journalism or forensics where you need a record of what an image contained.

Removal is where privacy is actually protected, and the tool offers precise control. You can strip all metadata at once, or selectively remove just the GPS location, the camera information (make, model, software), or the date and time — keeping the rest intact if you wish. For JPEG images, selective removal edits the metadata directly and re-inserts it, so the picture is not recompressed and its quality is preserved exactly. You can also choose the output format — keep the original, or convert to JPG, PNG or WebP — with a quality slider, and re-encoding through the canvas inherently drops every trace of metadata. When you have many photos to clean, batch processing lets you drop a whole set, strip them all in one go and download the results as a single ZIP, which is ideal before uploading a gallery, sending a batch of documents, or publishing product images.

Privacy is the entire point, so nothing about the tool undermines it. Every image you open, every tag it reads and every cleaned file it produces is handled entirely in your browser — no photo is ever uploaded to a server, there is no account or login, and nothing is tracked. This is fundamentally different from many "online" metadata removers that require you to upload your picture to their servers first; here, the sensitive image never leaves your device, which is exactly what you want when the whole purpose is to protect privacy. Because all processing is local, the tool is also fast and works offline once loaded, and it handles large photos smoothly by reading only the metadata it needs and using the browser's efficient canvas engine for re-encoding.

Finally, the tool follows modern standards of accessibility, responsiveness and maintainability. The dropzone and controls are keyboard-navigable and labelled for screen readers, focus outlines are visible, and high-contrast preferences are respected. The layout adapts from a spacious two-column preview-and-metadata view on desktop to a clean single column on tablets and phones, with touch-friendly controls and drag-and-drop everywhere. Internally, the parser configuration, privacy scoring, removal logic, export formats and batch handling are organised into small, well-defined functions, so the tool is accurate, easy to audit and simple to extend. In short, it is a fast, private, genuinely capable EXIF viewer and metadata remover suitable for everyday photo privacy and for professional imaging, journalism and security workflows alike.

🛠️ Troubleshooting

  • “No metadata found”: the image is already clean, or the format (e.g. many PNGs) doesn't store EXIF.
  • GPS still shows after removal: use “All metadata” or “GPS only”, then re-check the cleaned file.
  • Quality dropped: for JPEG, use a selective removal mode — it edits metadata without recompressing.
  • WebP output greyed out: some browsers don't support WebP encoding — choose JPG or PNG.
  • Large photo is slow: give it a moment; very large images take longer to re-encode.
  • Metadata library failed to load: check your connection and refresh — the parser loads from a CDN.
