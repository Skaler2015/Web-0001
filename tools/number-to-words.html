ℹ️ About This Tool

The Number to Words Converter is a fast, private, browser-based tool that turns any number or money amount into clear, correctly-spelled words — the way you need them for cheques, invoices, contracts, purchase orders, legal documents and school work. Writing an amount in words is a legal and practical necessity: a cheque is only valid when the amount appears in words as well as figures, an invoice reads more professionally with the total spelled out, and a contract removes ambiguity when a sum is written both ways. Doing this by hand is surprisingly error-prone — it is easy to miscount the zeros, drop a "lakh", or muddle "and" placement — and a single mistake on a cheque or agreement can cause real trouble. This tool removes that risk by generating the exact words instantly, and it does far more than a basic converter: it supports both numbering systems, two languages, ten currencies, several output styles, reverse conversion, batch processing and printable layouts.

A key strength is that it understands both numbering systems. The Indian system groups digits as thousand, lakh, crore, arab, kharab, neel, padma and shankh — so 12,34,567 reads as "Twelve Lakh Thirty Four Thousand Five Hundred Sixty Seven". The International system groups them as thousand, million, billion, trillion and onward to decillion, so the same figure reads as "One Million Two Hundred Thirty-Four Thousand Five Hundred Sixty-Seven". A long-scale variant is also available for regions where a billion means a million million. Because a cheque in India must use the Indian system while an international invoice uses millions and billions, being able to switch with one click matters. The engine uses exact big-integer arithmetic, so it stays accurate for very large numbers that would otherwise lose precision — right up to shankh and decillion.

The tool converts in English and Hindi, with the correct, idiomatic number words in each — including Hindi's famously irregular one-to-ninety-nine forms (इक्कीस, बत्तीस, उनतालीस and so on) that a naive converter gets wrong. For money, a dedicated currency mode spells amounts with the right units and sub-units for ten currencies — Indian Rupees & Paise, US/Australian/Canadian/Singapore Dollars & Cents, Euros & Cents, Pounds & Pence, UAE Dirhams & Fils, Saudi Riyals & Halalas, and Japanese Yen (which has no sub-unit) — and finishes with the customary "Only" so the output is cheque-ready. Decimals are handled two ways: as a spoken "point three five" for plain numbers, and as proper sub-units ("and Thirty Five Paise") in banking format for money.

Presentation is fully adjustable. You can switch the case to sentence case, Title Case, UPPER CASE (as banks often require) or lower case, and choose an output style: standard, British "and" placement (one hundred and one), hyphenated tens (twenty-one), or a strict banking style. A reverse converter goes the other way, parsing English words like "Two Crore Fifty Lakh" back into the digits 2,50,00,000 — useful for checking a document or digitising a written amount. A batch converter takes a whole list of numbers (typed or uploaded as CSV/TXT) and returns a table of words you can export, ideal for accountants preparing many cheques or invoices at once.

When it is time to use the result, the tool makes it effortless. You can copy just the words, just the number, or both together; print a plain result, a cheque layout or an invoice layout; and export to TXT, CSV, a Word-compatible DOC, or a PDF. A local history keeps your recent conversions and favorites in your browser so you can reuse them, and built-in examples and an educational guide explain the difference between crore and million, lakh and hundred-thousand, and how the two systems line up. Robust validation flags empty input, invalid characters, extreme values and malformed decimals, so you always get a trustworthy answer.

Because the entire tool is plain JavaScript running on your own device, it is lightweight and instant, works offline once the page has loaded, and is completely private — no number, amount or document detail you enter is ever uploaded to a server, there is no sign-up, and nothing is tracked against you. That privacy matters when the figures are financial. Who is it for? Bank customers and cashiers writing cheques; accountants and bookkeepers preparing invoices and vouchers; businesses issuing purchase orders and receipts; lawyers and notaries drafting agreements; teachers and students learning place value and the Indian and international systems; and anyone who simply needs a number spelled out correctly, once or in bulk, in English or Hindi.

🔢 Supported Number Systems, Languages & Currencies

Indian system: ones, tens, hundred, thousand, lakh (10⁵), crore (10⁷), arab (10⁹), kharab (10¹¹), neel (10¹³), padma (10¹⁵), shankh (10¹⁷).

International (short scale): thousand, million (10⁶), billion (10⁹), trillion (10¹²), quadrillion, quintillion, sextillion, septillion, octillion, nonillion, decillion (10³³). A long-scale option (billion = 10¹²) is also provided.

Languages: English and Hindi, each with correct number words. Currencies: INR, USD, EUR, GBP, AED, SAR, JPY, AUD, CAD, SGD — each with its proper main unit and sub-unit.

💳 Cheque-writing guide

  • Write the amount in words starting with the currency and ending with "Only" — e.g. "Rupees One Lakh Twenty Thousand Only". Use the Currency mode's banking style.
  • Match the figures box exactly; a mismatch between words and figures can void the cheque.
  • Draw a line after "Only" so no words can be added later.
  • For paise, write "…and Fifty Paise Only"; if there are none, end at "…Only".
  • Banks commonly prefer UPPER CASE — switch the Case option to match your bank's format.

📚 Educational guide — Indian vs International

  • Crore vs Million: 1 crore = 10 million; 10 crore = 100 million; 100 crore = 1 billion.
  • Lakh vs Hundred-thousand: 1 lakh = 100,000 = one hundred thousand.
  • Grouping: Indian places commas as 12,34,567 (groups of 2 after the first 3); International as 1,234,567 (groups of 3).
  • Arab & beyond: 1 arab = 100 crore = 1 billion; 1 kharab = 100 arab = 100 billion.

🛠️ Troubleshooting

  • Result stays "—": The input is empty or not a valid number. Remove letters and extra symbols; one decimal point is allowed.
  • Reads in millions instead of lakh: Switch the Number system to "Indian" (or vice-versa for International).
  • Currency words missing: Currency units only appear in the Currency mode; Plain Number mode spells the raw number.
  • Decimals look odd: In plain mode decimals are read digit-by-digit ("point three five"); in Currency mode they become sub-units ("and Thirty Five Paise").
  • Very large number is cut off: Beyond shankh (Indian) or decillion (International) the place names run out; the tool notes this — such magnitudes are rarely needed.
  • Reverse didn't parse: The reverse converter reads English words with scale terms (hundred, thousand, lakh, crore, million…). Check spelling and remove currency words.
  • History or favorites gone: They live in your browser only; clearing browser data or private mode will not retain them.
