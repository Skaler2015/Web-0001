ℹ️ About This Tool

Reversing text comes up in more situations than it might seem — mirror-writing a word for a design or puzzle, checking whether a word or phrase is a palindrome, flipping the order of items in a list, or just having a bit of fun turning a sentence backward to share with friends. Doing this character by character in your head or by hand is slow and easy to get wrong, especially with longer text.

This tool offers three different ways to reverse text depending on what you're after: flipping every individual character so the whole string reads backward, reversing the order of the words while keeping each word itself intact, or reversing the order of lines in a multi-line block of text.

How to use:

  1. Paste your text.
  2. Choose a reverse mode — characters, words, or lines.
  3. Click "Reverse," then copy the result.

The three modes explained

Character reversal turns "Hello World" into "dlroW olleH" — every single character flips order, which is the classic "mirror text" effect. Word reversal turns "Hello World" into "World Hello" — the words themselves stay spelled normally, but their order in the sentence flips, useful for testing word-order based puzzles or just rearranging a phrase. Line reversal takes a list or paragraph split across multiple lines and flips which line comes first and last, useful for reversing a numbered list, a set of steps, or any sequence where the line order matters but each line's content shouldn't change.

Palindrome checking is a popular use of character reversal — type in a word or short phrase, reverse it, and compare the two to see if they match (ignoring spaces and punctuation, a surprising number of phrases turn out to be palindromes once you account for that). Word reversal mode is handy for testing certain word games or puzzles, or for the simple novelty of seeing a familiar sentence read backward. Whatever the reason, all three modes work instantly on any length of text, from a single word to a full paragraph, with the result ready to copy as soon as you click reverse.

Teachers sometimes use reversed text to create simple decoding activities for younger students, while social media users enjoy posting a reversed sentence as a lighthearted puzzle for friends to figure out. Whatever the reason behind it, the conversion itself is completely lossless — reversing the same text twice always brings back the exact original, so there's no risk of losing or corrupting any part of the content along the way.
